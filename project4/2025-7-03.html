Overview

What if we treat pursuit evasion like a RL optimizable video game...

The rules of the game are simple: The evader (red) has the objective of reaching the green circle. The goal of the pursuer (blue) is to reach the evader before they reach the green circle

We can model this as a reinforcement learning problem where each agent learns to optimize their policy based on the rewards received for achieving their objectives.

The pursuers have information about their position ahd the evader's position. The evaders know of their position and that of the target green circle.

Iteratively, each side trains on a collection of the latest agent of the opposing side, some past agents, and default settings

Both sides learn strategies to thwart the other side. The evader might initially learn to just use a proportional controller to the green circle. However, the pursuer might easily stop that. Then, the evader might learn to use a more sophisticated strategy. Then train the pursuer and repeat

For example, at training iteration 6 of the pursuer, it trains on a collection of the latest evader, some past evaders, and default configurations like a wave-like evasion maneuver, random evasion maneuver, etc.

Tried Multi-agent Reinforcement Learning where multiple pursuers and evaders interact in this simulated environment. Tried different CTDE and DTDE approaches, however found little success.

Unknown why, but they didn't perform well. Perhaps due to the exponentially larger action spaces, training time, and computational complexity.

Future directions include more accurate dynamics to make the simulation more realistic and accounting for uncertainties in the environment and measurements
